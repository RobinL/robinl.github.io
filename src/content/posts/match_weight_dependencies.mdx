# Dependencies between match weights

The values of a column's match weights are interdependent: only certain combinations of values are possible. Some types of data also result in maximum and minimum match weights. Understanding these constraints can help you build better models and extract more information from comparison columns.

<NotebookCellProvider client:visible notebook="dependencies-between-match-weights">

## Example of a maximum match weight: Gender

Assume there is an equal split of males and females. A match on gender does not provide strong evidence because it is common amongst non-matching records. This gives an upper bound of 2.0 on the Bayes factor.

Amongst non-matching records, gender is just as likely to match as to mismatch: $u_{\text{gender, level 0}}=u_{\text{gender, level 1}}=0.5$.

### Observations

- There is a maximum bound of 2 on the match weight associated with a match on gender.
- There is no minimum bound on the weight associated with a mismatch. If gender is highly accurate, a mismatch is very strong evidence against a match.

## Example of a minimum match weight: Forename

Nicknames and diminutives mean that, amongst matches, forename may not exactly match. With only exact-match and mismatch levels, a mismatch therefore cannot provide very strong evidence against a match.

<NotebookCell cellId={5} />

Using a fuzzy matching level distinguishes similar names from completely different names:

- Level 2: exact match
- Level 1: fuzzy match
- Level 0: mismatch

## Observations

- With two levels, John versus Jonny receives the same negative weight as John versus Robin, losing information.
- With three levels, the mismatch level contains only completely different names, allowing a much stronger negative weight.
