A lot of it comes down to that they like a lot of meandering explanations about how to do stuff that Engelmann would just put in front of you and say: this is this. This is not this.

You could very quickly rule out the children’s interpretation to the point where they can learn it very quickly. I love this. Chris calls this categoricals. Engelmann called them comparatives and non-comparatives. Chris calls it categorical learning, where you just say yes and no.

If you think, how do I teach a child to regroup when I’m doing addition? Well, even before that, what DI programs do is go: this is correctly regrouped. This is not correctly regrouped. Which of these is correctly regrouped?

This is a yes or no, very, very simple task. I’m also aligning different things. Is this aligned properly in the columns? This is not aligned properly in the columns.

And then when I finally get to column addition, I have all of this conceptual and procedural knowledge that gets chained into this routine so that I can basically just do it all at once.

There’s this very interesting thing about really good design where you spend what seems like a very long amount of time on tool or pre-skill teaching, and all of a sudden it comes together and the kid just gets it first time and they feel awesome about themselves.

One of the things Daisy Christodoulis said to me a while ago, and I think she’s written about this, is that explanations are massively overrated.

You can spend five, 10 minutes doing an explanation, and there are a couple of issues with that. One, the kids are likely to be overwhelmed by it. Two, you’re getting no data back whatsoever. And three, you’ve just got a real inefficiency problem because you can cut all those words out.

As long as you present carefully sequenced examples and non-examples with minimal changes, or whatever it may be, and you’re careful with your selection, you can do a whole lot better in a lot less time.
